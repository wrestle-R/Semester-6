Week 7

Concepts

1. Decision-Making Levels in Organizations

Operational management deals with daily routine decisions such as payroll processing, transaction handling, and attendance recording. These decisions are repetitive and usually follow fixed rules.

Tactical management deals with medium-term planning such as campaign design, budgeting, and departmental strategy. These decisions use both rules and human judgment.

Strategic management focuses on long-term issues such as expansion, investment, and major policy decisions. These decisions are highly unstructured and require experience, judgment, and analysis.

2. Types of Decisions

Structured decisions have clear rules and can be repeated easily. For example, approving a standard reorder point in inventory can be structured.

Semi-structured decisions use both data and human interpretation. A marketing strategy often falls into this category.

Unstructured decisions do not have a clear formula. They require reasoning, judgment, and contextual understanding, such as approving a long-term capital investment.

3. BI Infrastructure and AI-Based Decision Support

A data warehouse is the core BI infrastructure component that stores organized data for reporting, dashboards, and analytics.

AI-based decision systems work best when the objective is clear, the constraints are known, and the possible alternatives are defined. When these conditions exist, AI can support or automate decisions more reliably.

An AI assistant usually responds to a user, explains information, or generates content on request. An AI agent goes further by autonomously executing tasks, selecting tools, and completing parts of a workflow.

4. Explainable AI and Reasoning Quality

Explainable AI (XAI) helps people understand how a model reached a decision.

SHAP and LIME are popular XAI tools. They explain a prediction by showing which features pushed the result upward or downward.

This is important because decision systems are more trustworthy when people can understand the reason behind the output.

5. Correlation, Causation, and Bias

Correlation means two things move together. Causation means one actually causes the other. Analysts must not confuse the two.

Anchoring bias happens when the first piece of information influences judgment too strongly. Confirmation bias happens when people only look for evidence that supports what they already believe. Overconfidence bias happens when people trust their own judgment too much.

6. Decision Trees

A decision tree is a supervised learning model that makes predictions through a series of if-then splits. Its goal is to create groups that are as pure or homogeneous as possible.

The root node is the starting point and represents the full dataset. A split divides the data based on a selected attribute. A leaf node gives the final prediction or class.

Induction means learning general rules from data examples.

7. Attribute Selection and Tree Growth

To choose the best attribute for a split, decision trees use measures such as Gini Index and Entropy. Lower impurity means a better split.

A greedy algorithm chooses the best local split at each step rather than searching for the globally perfect tree.

Overfitting happens when a tree becomes too detailed and memorizes the training data.

8. Pruning and Ensemble Methods

Pre-pruning stops the tree early, but it may miss useful splits. Post-pruning grows the tree fully and then cuts unnecessary branches, which is often better.

Bagging builds many trees in parallel and combines them by voting. Boosting builds trees sequentially, where each new model tries to correct the previous model’s mistakes.

Random Forest is a bagging-based method that also adds random feature selection. Bootstrapping means sampling with replacement to create many training datasets.

Example: In fraud detection, a decision tree may first split on transaction amount, then on country, and then on device type. Each split tries to make the fraud and non-fraud groups cleaner.

Week 8

Concepts

1. Decision Trees in Python Workflows

A decision tree is a flowchart-style predictive model. Each split tries to make the resulting groups more pure, meaning the observations inside each group become more similar in class.

A targeting problem means selecting the right customers, cases, or stores when resources are limited. The goal is to maximize results with a limited budget.

A pandas DataFrame is a table-like structure with rows and columns. It is commonly used to prepare and analyze data in Python.

scikit-learn is a Python library used to train models such as Decision Trees and Random Forests.

2. Training Data and Features

Train-test split divides the data into a training part and a testing part. The model learns patterns on the training data and is evaluated on the test data to see whether it generalizes.

Features (X) are the input variables, while target (Y) is the output the model tries to predict.

DecisionTree in scikit-learn generally expects numerical data, so text categories must be encoded or removed first.

Ordinal encoding is used when category order matters. For example, low, medium, and high can be converted to 0, 1, and 2.

3. Hyperparameters, Impurity, and Pruning

Hyperparameters are settings chosen before training. Examples include ccp_alpha and min_samples_leaf.

Gini impurity measures how mixed a node is. A value of 0 means the node is pure.

ccp_alpha controls pruning. A value of 0 means no pruning, while higher values create a smaller and simpler tree.

Overfitting happens when a tree memorizes the training data and performs poorly on unseen data.

.fit() is the method used to train the model.

4. Model Evaluation

A confusion matrix compares actual results with predicted results.

True Positive means a positive case was predicted correctly. True Negative means a negative case was predicted correctly. False Positive is a false alarm. False Negative is a missed positive case.

Recall or sensitivity measures how many actual positives were found. Specificity measures how well the model avoids false positives. Precision measures how many predicted positives are actually correct.

ROC curve plots True Positive Rate against False Positive Rate. AUC summarizes ROC performance, where 0.5 means random and 1.0 means perfect.

PR curve plots precision against recall and is especially useful for imbalanced data.

5. Imbalanced Data and Multi-Class Handling

Imbalanced data means one class is much larger than another. This can make the model biased toward the majority class.

SMOTE creates synthetic minority-class examples to balance the training data. It should be applied only to the training set, not to the test set.

LabelBinarizer converts multi-class targets into multiple binary forms so metrics can be computed more easily.

Micro averaging gives equal importance to all predictions. Macro averaging gives equal importance to all classes.

6. Random Forest and Cross-Validation

Random Forest combines many trees to improve stability and reduce sensitivity to one specific split.

Cross-validation uses repeated train-test splits to produce a more reliable estimate of model performance.

Example: If the dataset has 99 non-fraud cases and 1 fraud case, a model may look accurate by always predicting non-fraud. That is why recall, precision, PR curves, and SMOTE become important.

Week 9

Concepts

1. Risk Management Process

Risk management is the process of identifying risks, assessing them, and then controlling or reducing them. It is a structured way to protect systems from failure, harm, and loss.

Risk identification means finding possible threats such as data leakage, model failure, bias, or security attacks.

Risk assessment means estimating how likely a threat is and how serious the impact would be.

Risk control or mitigation means taking actions to reduce the probability or impact of those risks.

2. Quantitative Risk Assessment

Quantitative risk assessment uses numbers rather than only qualitative judgment.

A common framework treats risk as a function of threat probability, success probability of attack, exposure of assets, and asset value.

Measurement error is the uncertainty or inaccuracy in estimating these values.

3. Levels of Risk Assessment

Risk can be assessed at the individual level, the organizational level, or the national level. The same technical failure may have very different consequences at each level.

A measurable impact of AI system failure is often financial cost, such as the cost created by a wrong automated decision.

4. Threat Categories and ML Attacks

Threats to information systems can be broadly classified as malicious or unintentional.

Malicious threats are intentional attacks. Unintentional threats come from accidents, mistakes, bugs, or poor design.

Data poisoning is a malicious attack where the training data is corrupted so the model learns wrong patterns.

Adversarial input attacks slightly modify inputs to cause wrong predictions, even though the changes may be hard for humans to notice.

5. Environmental Concerns and Bias

Large AI models consume a great deal of energy during training and deployment. This creates environmental concern because of high electricity use and carbon emissions.

A common criticism of large-scale AI systems is that the training data may not represent the diversity of the real population. This can lead to bias and unfair outputs.

Example: If a face recognition model is trained mostly on one group of faces, it may perform worse on underrepresented groups. This becomes a fairness and risk management problem at the same time.

Week 10

Concepts

1. Clustering and Store Segmentation

Clustering is an unsupervised learning method that groups similar observations together without pre-labeled classes.

Store segmentation applies clustering to stores instead of customers so that similar stores can be managed using similar strategies.

Cross-sectional data is data collected at one point in time across many units. This is important because clustering should not confuse time-based change with true similarity.

Cluster profiling means interpreting the meaning of each cluster by examining average values and business characteristics.

2. K-Means and Data Preparation

K-Means partitions observations into K clusters based on distance from centroids. It works best when features are scaled and K is chosen carefully.

Multicollinearity means variables are highly correlated and duplicate information. Redundant variables can distort clustering.

Scaling using a method such as StandardScaler is important because K-Means is distance-based, and large numeric values can dominate the result.

Elbow method plots SSE against K. The bend or elbow point suggests a good number of clusters.

SSE, or Sum of Squared Errors, measures how tight the clusters are. Lower SSE generally means better clustering.

3. RFM Analysis and Customer Value

RFM analysis segments customers using Recency, Frequency, and Monetary value.

Recency means how recently the customer purchased. Lower time since the last purchase is better and often the strongest indicator of current engagement.

Frequency measures how often the customer purchases. High frequency often signals loyalty.

Monetary measures how much the customer spent. It indicates revenue contribution.

CLV is customer lifetime value, the predicted future value of a customer. RFM helps estimate it.

4. Customer Segment Meanings

True Friends have high recency quality, high frequency, and high monetary value. They are strong loyal customers.

Butterflies spend a lot but are not very frequent. The firm should maximize short-term profit from them.

Barnacles buy often but spend little. The strategy is to increase their average spend.

Strangers have low values across RFM and often do not deserve major investment.

5. Data Cleaning and Aggregation

Data cleaning removes errors such as impossible or negative values that should not exist in standard purchases.

Returns are treated as negative transactions so they reduce total revenue.

Pivot tables are used to aggregate RFM values: SUM for monetary, COUNT for frequency, and MAX date for recency calculations.

Scaling can also convert raw RFM values into a common score range such as 1 to 10. Reverse scaling is often used for recency because lower days since purchase should produce a higher score.

NTILE(n) divides data into equal-sized groups such as quintiles or deciles.

fit_predict() trains a model and returns cluster labels in one step.

3D visualization is useful in RFM because there are three main features to display.

6. LLM and Voice Assistant Topics in Online Assessment

This week’s online assessment also touches separate AI application topics such as LLMs, RAG, prompt injection, vector similarity, voice assistants, and agentic workflows. These are included in the assessment section exactly as provided.

Example: If a customer bought very recently, buys often, and spends a lot, the customer may be tagged as a True Friend. If another customer spends a lot once in a while, that customer fits better in the Butterfly segment.

Week 11

Concepts

1. Fairness, Diversity, and Inclusion

Diversity means having people from different backgrounds, identities, and communities represented in a group.

Inclusion means making sure those people are not only present but also valued, heard, and able to participate meaningfully.

Systematic relative disadvantage means some groups repeatedly face unfair treatment because of bias built into social systems.

Equality of opportunity in the middle-view sense recognizes past disadvantage and tries to create fair chances rather than pretending all groups started from the same position.

2. Responsibility and Accountability in AI

The responsibility gap appears because AI systems can make impactful decisions without having consciousness or moral agency, while humans may not fully control every outcome.

Distributed responsibility means responsibility is shared across developers, firms, deployers, users, regulators, and other stakeholders.

3. AI Risk Management Responses

Defense means using safeguards to prevent or block harmful behavior.

Mitigation means reducing the impact when harm cannot be fully prevented.

Transfer means shifting risk to another party, such as through insurance, outsourcing, or contractual arrangements.

Acceptance means deciding to tolerate a risk when it is low, unavoidable, or not worth the cost of prevention.

4. AI Evaluation Measures

Language Modeling Score (LMS) measures how often a model chooses a sensible or logical answer instead of a nonsensical one.

Stereotype Score (SS) measures how often a model chooses stereotyped responses. A higher stereotype score implies more bias.

5. ANN Concepts

ANN training adjusts weights to reduce prediction error.

Backpropagation sends error backward through the network so the weights can be updated.

A feedforward network moves information from input to output without loops. Hidden layers help the model learn more complex patterns.

The bias-variance tradeoff still matters: a model that is too simple underfits, while a model that is too complex overfits.

6. Time Series and Financial Signals

Time series data is collected across time intervals, such as daily stock prices or hourly demand.

Technical analysis uses past prices and trading patterns to estimate future movement, while fundamental analysis uses broader economic and company information.

RSI is a momentum indicator. A very high RSI may signal overbought conditions, while a very low RSI may signal oversold conditions.

7. Implementation Concepts

Data partitioning in time series should use older data for training and newer data for testing to avoid look-ahead bias.

Feature engineering creates useful variables such as SMA, RSI, and momentum.

Data synchronization ensures that all time-based features line up correctly on the same timeline before modeling.

Example: If a trading model uses tomorrow’s price while pretending to predict today’s movement, it is cheating. Proper time-series partitioning prevents this kind of look-ahead bias.

Week 12

Concepts

1. Text Mining Basics

Text mining means converting messy text such as reviews, emails, posts, or documents into structured data that a machine can analyze.

Unstructured data is data without a fixed tabular format. Free text is the main example.

The core idea is simple: first structure the text, then analyze it.

2. Lexical and Semantic Approaches

Lexical methods such as Bag-of-Words focus on word presence and frequency but ignore order and deeper meaning.

Semantic analysis tries to understand meaning and context rather than only counting terms.

NLP teaches computers to process human language, while linguistics provides the theory of grammar, meaning, and language structure.

3. POS Tagging and Core Text Terms

POS tagging labels words as noun, verb, adjective, and so on. Nouns often indicate product features or topics, while adjectives often signal sentiment.

A corpus is the full text dataset. A document is one unit inside the corpus, such as one review or one tweet.

A token is a small unit such as a word or symbol. Tokenization is the process of splitting text into tokens.

4. Cleaning and Structuring Text

Stop words such as “the” and “is” are often removed because they add little meaning.

Stemming reduces words to a root form, such as turning “loved” into a stem like “lov”.

A Term-Document Matrix (TDM) turns text into numbers, where rows represent documents, columns represent terms, and values represent frequency.

n-grams capture word combinations. A bigram has two words, such as “data mining”.

5. Importance, Similarity, and Sentiment

TF measures term frequency in one document. IDF penalizes terms that appear in many documents. TF-IDF highlights words that are important in one document but not common everywhere.

Cosine similarity compares documents using angle-based similarity. A value closer to 1 means the documents are more similar.

Phi coefficient measures whether words tend to appear together.

Sentiment analysis uses lexicons such as AFINN or NRC to classify language as positive, negative, or emotionally meaningful.

6. R and Tidy Text Concepts

Tidy format means each row is one observation and each column is one variable.

A tibble is an improved data frame in R.

unnest_tokens() performs tokenization. anti_join() helps remove stop words. SnowballC is used for stemming. widyr is used for correlation analysis.

LLMs are advanced language models built on transformers and deep learning. They go beyond simple lexical methods and can model context at scale.

Example: If many movie reviews contain the word “boring,” TF-IDF may highlight it in a specific negative review, and sentiment analysis may classify the review as negative.
