Week 1

Concepts

1. Foundational Concepts of BI&A

Business Intelligence and Analytics (BI&A) is the ability to convert raw data into useful intelligence that supports better business decisions. Instead of depending on guesswork, companies use BI&A to make choices based on evidence and strategy.

Informed decisions are made using data, analysis, and clear goals. Uninformed decisions depend on luck, intuition, or assumptions. The purpose of BI&A is to reduce uncertainty and move decisions toward the informed side.

User-generated data is data created by users while using digital platforms. Searches on Google, product views on Amazon, clicks on websites, and app activity are all examples. Many companies provide free services because the data created by users becomes highly valuable for analytics.

Analytics is the deeper engine inside BI&A. BI helps organize, store, and report data, while analytics uses algorithms and models to discover patterns, explain behavior, and predict future outcomes.

Data as a strategic asset means that data itself creates business value. In modern digital businesses, a company can be worth billions not only because of current revenue, but because of the user data, network connections, and future opportunities that data makes possible.

2. Patterns and the Data Revolution

Descriptive analytics answers the question, “What is happening?” It summarizes historical data and gives a factual picture of business performance. For example, a monthly sales report is descriptive analytics.

Explanatory or diagnostic analytics asks, “Why is it happening?” It goes beyond observation and tests possible reasons behind a pattern. For example, if sales drop in one region, the analyst studies pricing, competition, and customer behavior to explain the cause.

Predictive analytics asks, “What is likely to happen next?” It uses past data and models to forecast future outcomes such as churn, sales, or fraud risk.

Prescriptive analytics asks, “What should we do?” It recommends actions based on predicted outcomes. For example, it may suggest giving a retention offer to customers with high churn risk.

Cognitive analytics uses AI-like reasoning to simulate more human-like decision support, especially when handling complex or unstructured information.

A hypothesis is a testable explanation proposed from limited evidence. It helps analysts move from noticing a pattern to examining whether a real cause exists.

The Long Tail describes a business model in which many low-demand niche items together can generate as much value as a few best-selling items. This became practical because digital platforms make storage, search, and distribution much cheaper than in physical stores.

Macro-forces behind the data revolution are the falling costs of processing power, data storage, and data transmission. These three forces made it possible to work with massive amounts of data.

3. BI&A Architecture and Course Pillars

BI&A architecture is the technical setup that stores, moves, organizes, and analyzes data. It includes databases, data warehouses, ETL systems, and OLAP systems. It acts like the factory where raw data is prepared for analysis.

Business-to-analytics translation is the skill of converting a vague business problem into a clear data problem. For example, “sales are down” must be translated into measurable questions such as which products, which customers, which regions, and during which time period are responsible.

Machine learning refers to methods that learn patterns from data and use them for prediction or classification. In this course context, it is closely related to data mining.

The course learning objectives move from understanding architecture, to translating business problems, to applying techniques, and finally to using practical software tools.

Software tools such as Python, Jupyter Notebook, R, RStudio, and SQL are used to work with data, run analyses, and apply models on large datasets.

4. Vocabulary of Analytics

ETL stands for Extract, Transform, Load. First, data is extracted from sources. Then it is transformed by cleaning, formatting, and standardizing it. Finally, it is loaded into a data warehouse or target system.

Data warehousing is the practice of storing cleaned, integrated, structured data in a central place for analysis. A warehouse is designed for historical queries and decision support rather than daily operations.

OLAP, or Online Analytical Processing, supports multidimensional analysis. It lets managers slice and dice data by different dimensions such as region, time, product, or customer segment.

Data mining, also called KDD or Knowledge Discovery in Databases, is the process of finding hidden patterns, relationships, and useful knowledge in large datasets.

Big Data is data characterized by high volume, velocity, and variety. Traditional tools struggle to handle it, so specialized systems are often required.

The three pillars of success in analytics are technology, science, and domain knowledge. Good tools alone are not enough. The analyst also needs statistical thinking and understanding of the business context.

Example: A company may have millions of customer clicks. BI organizes that information into dashboards, while analytics studies it to predict which customers are likely to buy, leave, or respond to an offer.

Concepts

1. Bizocity Case and Analytics

Customer acquisition means getting new customers. In the Bizocity case, older methods such as random calling wasted time and money because many contacted numbers were not useful leads.

As-Is describes the current or old system. To-Be describes the improved future system. In this case, the old method depended on manual calling, while the better method used data to target likely business customers.

Call Detail Data (CDD) contains metadata about calls, such as the numbers involved, time of call, and duration. It does not include the actual voice content of the call.

Bizocity score is a score between 0 and 1 that indicates how likely a phone number belongs to a business. A higher score means the number behaves more like a business number.

Behavior indicators are clues from usage patterns. Business numbers often show daytime calling, longer calls, and interactions with other business-like numbers.

RFM analysis studies behavior using Recency, Frequency, and Monetary value. In this context, recency means how recently the number was seen, frequency means how often it appeared, and monetary value is represented by call duration.

The key idea is that we do not always need to ask directly whether a customer is a good target. Sometimes behavior itself gives a strong signal.

2. Business Intelligence Architecture

BI architecture is the full system that takes raw data, prepares it, analyzes it, and turns it into decision support.

OLTP stands for Online Transaction Processing. These systems handle daily operations such as orders, payments, and bookings. They are designed for fast and frequent transaction processing.

ETL prepares data by extracting it from sources, transforming it into clean and consistent form, and loading it into a storage system designed for analysis.

A data warehouse stores integrated, historical, structured data for analysis.

OLAP helps analyze the stored data from different dimensions.

Data mining finds patterns and makes predictions.

A data mart is a smaller part of a warehouse created for one department or business function, such as marketing or finance.

A data lake stores raw data, including unstructured data such as social media posts, video, audio, and logs.

Inmon’s approach is top-down: build the enterprise warehouse first, then create smaller marts. Kimball’s approach is bottom-up: build smaller data marts first and integrate them later.

3. Data Management and RDBMS

Data management means handling data carefully so that it remains correct, complete, useful, and consistent.

RDBMS, or Relational Database Management System, stores data in tables linked by relationships.

An entity is a real-world object represented in data, such as a customer, product, or order.

An attribute is a property of an entity, such as customer name, product price, or order date.

A primary key uniquely identifies each row in a table and cannot be duplicated or null.

A foreign key connects one table to another by referencing a key in the related table.

Entity integrity ensures that each record is uniquely identifiable. Referential integrity ensures that relationships remain valid. Data integrity means that data remains accurate and consistent overall.

4. OLTP and Database Design

Cardinality describes the type of relationship between entities, such as one-to-one, one-to-many, or many-to-many.

In a one-to-many relationship, the foreign key is placed on the many side. For example, one customer can place many orders, so the customer key appears in the order table.

A many-to-many relationship cannot usually be implemented directly. It needs an associative entity or junction table to connect the two sides.

Normalization removes repetition and improves consistency.

ACID properties make transactions reliable: Atomicity means all or nothing, Consistency means valid state, Isolation means transactions do not disturb one another, and Durability means committed changes are permanently saved.

OLTP vs data warehouse: OLTP is for current daily work; the warehouse is for historical analysis.

Example: A telecom company can examine daytime calling frequency and call duration to decide whether a number is likely to belong to a business, without ever listening to the call itself.

Week 3

Concepts

1. DBMS, RDBMS, and SQL Basics

DBMS is a system that stores and manages data. RDBMS is a relational DBMS that stores data in tables and connects those tables using relationships.

SQL is the language used to communicate with relational databases. It can be used to retrieve, add, update, and structure data. SQL commands are generally not case-sensitive, so select and SELECT mean the same thing.

In a table, a row is one full record, while a column is one field or attribute.

2. ACID and Keys

ACID properties make transactions reliable. Atomicity means a transaction happens completely or not at all. Consistency means the database remains valid. Isolation means concurrent transactions do not interfere with each other. Durability means once a transaction is committed, it stays saved even after a failure.

A super key is any set of attributes that uniquely identifies a row. A candidate key is a minimal super key. A primary key is the chosen candidate key used as the main identifier. A foreign key links one table to another.

A composite key uses multiple columns together to identify a row. A compound key is a type of composite key that includes at least one foreign key.

3. SQL Commands and JOINs

DDL or Data Definition Language is used for structure, such as CREATE and ALTER.

DML or Data Manipulation Language is used to insert and modify data, such as INSERT and UPDATE.

DQL or Data Query Language is used to retrieve data, mainly through SELECT.

INNER JOIN returns matching rows from both tables. LEFT JOIN returns all rows from the left table and matching rows from the right. RIGHT JOIN returns all rows from the right table and matching rows from the left. FULL JOIN returns all rows from both sides.

4. Normalization

Normalization is the process of organizing data to reduce redundancy and avoid anomalies.

Insertion anomaly happens when you cannot add data properly without adding unrelated data. Deletion anomaly happens when deleting one fact removes another useful fact. Update anomaly happens when the same value is stored in many places and is not updated consistently.

First Normal Form (1NF) requires each cell to contain only one value.

Second Normal Form (2NF) removes partial dependency, meaning a non-key attribute should depend on the full key, not part of it.

Third Normal Form (3NF) removes transitive dependency, meaning non-key attributes should not depend on other non-key attributes.

BCNF is a stronger version of 3NF in which the determinant on the left side of every dependency must be a super key.

The trade-off is that higher normalization improves cleanliness and integrity, but queries may become slower because more joins are needed.

5. OLAP Concepts

OLTP handles transactional work. OLAP is designed for analysis and reporting.

Metadata is data about data, such as table names, column names, and data types.

A fact table stores measurable values like sales amount, quantity, or profit. A dimension table stores descriptive context like time, product, or location.

A star schema is denormalized and has fewer joins, so it is often faster for analysis. A snowflake schema is more normalized and uses more joins.

A data cube stores data in multiple dimensions. Common operations are slice for one fixed value, dice for a range or subset, drill-down for more detail, roll-up for summary, and pivot for changing the view.

Materialization means precomputing and storing results in advance so queries can run faster later.

Example: In a sales cube, you may analyze sales by time, product, and city. Viewing only one city is slicing, while looking at selected cities and products together is dicing.

Concepts

1. Customer Churn and Survival Analysis

Customer churn happens when a customer stops using a product or service. If someone cancels a subscription or becomes inactive, that customer has churned.

Survival analysis studies the time until an event occurs. In marketing and customer management, the event is often churn. Instead of looking only at who left, survival analysis also looks at when they left.

Customer tenure is the length of time a customer stays with the company. Higher tenure usually means greater long-term value.

The half-life period is the time by which 50% of customers have churned. A short half-life suggests weak retention.

2. Why Retention Matters

Companies spend money to acquire customers. If customers leave quickly, the firm may not recover that cost. This is why retention is often more profitable than constantly acquiring new customers.

A small reduction in churn can lead to a large increase in profit because loyal customers tend to buy more over time, cost less to serve, and may bring referrals.

3. Tenure Timeline, Hazard Rate, and Survival Rate

To compare customers fairly, analysts often use a tenure timeline. Instead of using calendar dates like January or February, they use Month 0, Month 1, Month 2, and so on, starting from the customer’s join date.

Hazard rate H(t) is the probability that a customer leaves during a specific period, given that the customer has survived up to that period. It tells us the risk of churn “now.”

Survival rate S(t) is the probability that a customer is still active at time t. Survival usually starts at 1 and decreases or stays the same over time.

4. CLV and Customer Equity

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) is the total expected future profit from a customer.

A discount rate is applied because money expected in the future is worth less than money received today.

Customer equity is the total value of all customers together, which is the sum of their CLVs.

CLV is a forward-looking measure. It helps businesses decide how much to spend on acquisition, retention, and customer service.

5. Key Business Insight

The biggest profitability improvement often comes from increasing retention. Keeping customers longer increases tenure, increases CLV, and raises overall customer equity.

Example: If a streaming customer stays for 24 months instead of 6 months, the company earns more subscription revenue and has more time to recover acquisition cost and generate profit.

Concepts

1. Analytics as a Process

Analytics is not just about tools. It is a structured process for turning a business problem into a useful answer. If the process is weak, the result will also be weak.

Mindfulness means questioning the data, the assumptions, and the output. An analyst should not blindly trust software or numbers.

Parsimony, also called Occam’s Razor, means using the simplest model that works well. Too many variables can create noise, confusion, and overfitting.

2. Six-Step Analytics Lifecycle

The usual lifecycle is: define the problem, select variables, collect data, build the model, test the model, and implement and improve the solution.

The thought process is the most important step because it defines the business question, the output, and the approach.

3. Variable Selection and Data Quality

Variables can be chosen from prior research, domain experts, business usefulness, control needs, simplicity, and data availability.

Multicollinearity happens when variables are highly related to each other, such as age and date of birth. This can confuse the model.

A rough warning sign is correlation above 0.7. Another sign is a high VIF, where a value above 2 is considered problematic in these notes. The formula is VIF = 1 / (1 - R²).

Data quality includes accuracy, completeness, consistency, timeliness, believability, and interpretability.

Data should also be inspected manually. A strange value like zero sales may be a real event or a system error, so it should be checked before modeling.

4. Models, Learning, and Fit

A model is a simplified representation of reality. It tries to explain or predict an outcome using input variables.

Supervised learning uses labeled output variables. Unsupervised learning does not have labeled output and instead discovers patterns such as clusters.

RSS or Residual Sum of Squares measures the difference between actual and predicted values. Lower RSS means a better fit.

R² shows how much variation the model explains. It lies between 0 and 1. Higher values usually mean a better fit.

Standardization using Z-scores converts variables to a common scale with mean 0 and standard deviation 1. This helps especially in explanatory models.

Explanatory models focus on understanding why something happens. Predictive models focus on making accurate predictions, even if interpretation is less important.

5. Bias, Variance, and Validation

Bias comes from overly simple models and leads to underfitting. Variance comes from overly complex models that react too strongly to noise and leads to overfitting.

The bias-variance tradeoff means good modeling requires balance between simplicity and flexibility.

Training error is the error on data used to build the model. Test error is the error on unseen data and is more important for real performance.

Cross-validation helps estimate performance more reliably. In K-fold cross-validation, data is split into K parts and tested repeatedly. In LOOCV, K equals the number of observations.

6. Business Examples

The XYZ Bank case illustrates profitability skew, where a small percentage of customers contribute most of the profit.

Dummy regression uses 0 and 1 variables to represent categories and compare groups.

Correlation means variables move together. Causation means one variable actually causes the other. Confusing these two is a major analytical mistake.

Example: Ice cream sales and drowning cases may both rise in summer. They are correlated, but ice cream does not cause drowning. The real driver is hot weather.

Concepts

1. Classification and Targeting

Classification is the process of assigning observations to predefined classes, such as spam or not spam, likely buyer or unlikely buyer, churn or no churn. Because the correct class labels are known in training data, classification is a form of supervised learning.

Predefined classes are what make classification different from clustering. In classification, the categories already exist. In clustering, the groups are discovered from data.

A targeting problem is a common business use of classification. The goal is to identify the best subgroup, such as customers most likely to respond to a campaign.

Lift shows how much better a model performs compared with random selection. A lift greater than 1 means the model is useful.

2. Classification Algorithms

Logistic regression produces probabilities between 0 and 1. It is widely used for binary classification.

Naive Bayes is a probability-based classifier built on Bayes’ theorem. It is simple and fast.

Discriminant analysis tries to find the boundary that separates classes well.

Decision trees split data step by step using rules, which makes them easy to understand.

AI methods such as Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) and Support Vector Machines (SVM) can capture complex patterns.

Clustering is normally unsupervised, but once clusters are labeled, they may sometimes be used in a classification-like way.

3. Model Evaluation and Confusion Matrix

Model fit shows how well the model explains the training data, but this alone is not enough.

Prediction performance shows how well the model works on new data, which matters more in practice.

A confusion matrix summarizes predictions using four outcomes: True Positive, True Negative, False Positive, and False Negative.

Accuracy is the proportion of all correct predictions. Sensitivity or recall measures how well the model finds actual positives. Specificity measures how well it identifies actual negatives. Precision measures how correct the positive predictions are. F1 score balances precision and recall.

4. ROC, PR, Gains, and Lift

A gains chart shows how effectively the model captures positives when observations are ranked.

A ROC curve plots True Positive Rate against False Positive Rate. A better model stays closer to the top-left corner.

AUC is the area under the ROC curve. A value of 0.5 means random performance, while 1.0 means perfect performance.

A PR curve plots precision against recall and is especially useful for imbalanced datasets.

5. Class Imbalance, Sampling, and Thresholds

Class imbalance happens when one class is much smaller than the other, such as fraud vs non-fraud. In such cases, accuracy can be misleading.

Over-sampling increases minority class representation. Under-sampling reduces the majority class. SMOTE creates synthetic minority examples.

The threshold converts predicted probabilities into class labels. A lower threshold gives more positive predictions and may increase false positives. A higher threshold gives fewer positive predictions and may increase false negatives.

6. Business Interpretation

The best metric depends on the business problem. In disease detection, missing a true case is dangerous, so sensitivity matters. In loan approval or fraud screening, false positives may be expensive, so specificity matters. In search or recommendation, precision may matter more.

Example: If only 1 out of 100 customers buys a product, a model that predicts “no” for everyone gets 99% accuracy, but it is still useless. This is why accuracy alone is not enough for imbalanced data.
